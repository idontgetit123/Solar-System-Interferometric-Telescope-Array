# FAQ.md
## Solar System Baseline Interferometer (SSBI)
### Frequently Asked Questions

---

## ⭐ Overview
This FAQ addresses the most common questions about the Solar System Baseline Interferometer (SSBI), its capabilities, limitations, and long‑term vision. It is designed for scientists, engineers, students, and anyone curious about how a **solar‑system‑scale telescope** actually works.

---

## ⭐ 1. What is SSBI?
SSBI is a distributed interferometric telescope that uses **AU‑scale baselines** to directly image the surfaces of exoplanets within 20–50 light‑years.  
It turns the entire Solar System into a **single optical instrument**.

---

## ⭐ 2. How does SSBI achieve such high resolution?
Resolution in interferometry depends on **baseline length**, not mirror size.  
SSBI uses:

- 1 AU baselines (150 million km)  
- multi‑ring orbital geometry  
- synchronized photon capture  
- Fourier synthesis  

This produces **continent‑level detail** on nearby exoplanets.

---

## ⭐ 3. How many satellites are required?
The system is modular. A functional array can begin with:

- **dozens** of satellites (minimum)  
- scaling to **hundreds** for full Fourier coverage  

More satellites = sharper images.

---

## ⭐ 4. Can SSBI detect life?
SSBI does **not** directly detect biosignatures.  
But it provides:

- surface maps  
- climate cycles  
- ocean/land distribution  
- seasonal color shifts  

These contextual clues support biosignature interpretation when combined with spectroscopy.

---

## ⭐ 5. What wavelengths does SSBI use?
SSBI operates across:

- **Visible light** (surface detail)  
- **Near‑infrared** (vegetation‑like reflectance, heat)  
- **Mid‑infrared** (thermal emission, atmospheric windows)  

Future upgrades may include UV and far‑IR.

---

## ⭐ 6. How does SSBI maintain timing across AU‑scale distances?
Using:

- sub‑picosecond atomic clocks  
- optical laser time transfer  
- relativistic drift correction  
- predictive phase modeling  

This ensures phase coherence across millions of kilometers.

---

## ⭐ 7. How long does it take to reconstruct an image?
Reconstruction depends on:

- number of baselines  
- wavelength bands  
- exposure time  
- GPU cluster size  

Typical processing: **hours to days**.

---

## ⭐ 8. Can SSBI operate autonomously?
Yes.  
SSBI uses:

- AI‑assisted navigation  
- autonomous station‑keeping  
- fault‑tolerant control systems  
- self‑calibration routines  

Human oversight is minimal.

---

## ⭐ 9. What happens if a satellite fails?
The system is redundant.  
If a satellite fails:

- baselines are recalculated  
- Fourier plane coverage adjusts  
- reconstruction algorithms compensate  

The array continues operating with reduced resolution.

---

## ⭐ 10. Can SSBI be expanded beyond the Solar System?
Yes — this is part of the long‑term vision.

Future **cosmic nodes** in other star systems (e.g., Proxima Centauri) can synchronize clocks with Earth and perform **interstellar interferometry**, creating multi‑light‑year baselines.

This enables imaging of planets **hundreds of light‑years away**.

---

## ⭐ 11. How does two‑star interferometry work?
Two SSBI arrays — one in the Solar System, one in Proxima — each capture data locally.  
After multi‑year light‑time delays, the datasets are combined using:

- predictive timing envelopes  
- phase‑closure correction  
- joint Fourier synthesis  

This creates the largest baseline in human history.

---

## ⭐ 12. What is the Exoplanet Atlas?
A long‑term archive containing:

- surface maps  
- climate cycles  
- atmospheric evolution  
- rotation and seasonal data  

It is the first attempt at **extrasolar cartography**.
