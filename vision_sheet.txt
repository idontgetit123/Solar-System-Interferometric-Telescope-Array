The Solar System Baseline Interferometer (SSBI)

A James‑Origin Future Imaging Architecture

⭐ Vision

Humanity’s first solar‑system‑scale camera — a distributed array of autonomous satellites positioned across AU‑scale baselines, using optical and infrared interferometry to reconstruct continent‑level images of exoplanets within 20–50 light‑years.

This is the moment we stop guessing what other worlds look like…
and start seeing them.

⭐ Core Concept

A network of satellites arranged across orbital rings around the Sun, each equipped with:

high‑precision optical/IR collectors

ultra‑stable clocks

deep‑space laser links

onboard preprocessing

synchronized photon capture

Data is transmitted to a GPU cluster that performs aperture synthesis, reconstructing full‑resolution planetary images from multi‑baseline interference patterns.

This turns the entire solar system into a single telescope mirror.

⭐ Why It Works

Resolution in interferometry is determined by baseline, not mirror size.

LIFE baseline: 100–500 m

JWST mirror: 6.5 m

Your array baseline: 1 AU (150,000,000 km)

This is a million‑fold jump in resolving power.

At AU‑scale baselines, we can theoretically resolve:

continents

oceans

cloud systems

seasonal changes

rotation curves

atmospheric scattering patterns

on planets orbiting nearby stars.

⭐ Scientific Impact

This system enables:

direct imaging of Earth‑like planets

mapping of exoplanet surfaces

detection of oceans, landmasses, ice caps

weather and climate monitoring

atmospheric composition analysis

long‑term habitability studies

This is the first technology capable of answering:

“What do other Earths actually look like?”

A permanent record of the worlds around us.
